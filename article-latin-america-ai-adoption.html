Latin American Companies Are Embracing AI at an Accelerating Pace

By Lucia Fernandez • March 8, 2026

Latin America is experiencing a quiet AI revolution. While global attention often focuses on AI development in the United States, China, and Europe, companies across Brazil, Mexico, Colombia, and other regional economies are adopting artificial intelligence at rates that are beginning to rival more developed markets. This adoption is being driven by a unique combination of factors: young, digitally-native populations; mobile-first economies that skipped legacy infrastructure; and a new generation of entrepreneurs who see AI as an opportunity to solve distinctly Latin American challenges.

The fintech sector has been an early and aggressive AI adopter. In a region where large portions of the population remain underbanked, AI-powered credit scoring systems that can evaluate borrowers without traditional credit histories have enabled companies like Nubank and Creditas to extend financial services to millions of previously excluded consumers. These same capabilities are now expanding into insurance, where AI models assess risk for populations that legacy actuarial tables cannot adequately serve, and into investment services that make sophisticated portfolio management accessible to retail investors.

Agriculture, a cornerstone of several Latin American economies, is being transformed by AI applications that address the specific challenges of tropical and subtropical farming. Brazilian agtech startups are deploying computer vision systems for pest detection, satellite imagery analysis for crop health monitoring, and machine learning models that optimize fertilizer and water usage for local conditions. These tools are helping farmers increase yields while reducing environmental impact—a critical balance in a region that contains much of the world's remaining arable land and biodiversity.

The healthcare sector presents both significant opportunities and unique challenges. AI diagnostic tools developed for North American and European populations often perform poorly on Latin American patient populations due to differences in disease prevalence, genetic backgrounds, and healthcare infrastructure. Local companies are responding by developing AI systems trained on regional data, addressing conditions that are common locally but underrepresented in global training datasets. Telemedicine platforms powered by AI are extending specialist consultations to rural areas where such expertise was previously unavailable.

Investment in the region's AI ecosystem is growing rapidly. Venture capital firms from both inside and outside Latin America are increasing their allocations to AI-focused startups. Governments are launching national AI strategies, with Brazil, Chile, and Colombia all announcing significant initiatives in the past year. Several leading universities are establishing AI research centers, and multinational technology companies are opening regional AI labs to tap into local talent and develop products suited to local markets.

Challenges remain significant. The region faces a shortage of trained AI professionals, though numerous bootcamps and university programs are working to close this gap. Infrastructure limitations, particularly in connectivity and computing resources, constrain what is possible in some areas. Regulatory frameworks for AI remain underdeveloped, creating uncertainty for companies navigating questions of liability, privacy, and algorithmic accountability. Economic volatility in some countries complicates long-term planning and investment.

Despite these obstacles, the momentum behind AI adoption in Latin America appears durable. The technology is proving its value in addressing real problems faced by businesses and consumers in the region. As more success stories emerge and as the ecosystem of talent, capital, and supporting infrastructure matures, Latin America is positioning itself not just as a consumer of AI developed elsewhere, but as a significant contributor to the global development of artificial intelligence—with applications that may prove valuable far beyond its borders.
