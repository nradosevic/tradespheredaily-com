How AI Is Changing Technical Hiring: New Interview Practices Emerge

By Jennifer Walsh • February 24, 2026

The technical interview, long a cornerstone of engineering hiring, is being fundamentally disrupted by artificial intelligence. Candidates are using AI coding assistants during take-home assignments and even during live interviews. Companies are discovering that traditional coding challenges no longer reliably assess the skills that matter in a world where AI can write boilerplate code. And hiring managers are grappling with a more fundamental question: if engineers will work with AI tools daily on the job, shouldn't interviews allow—or even require—their use?

The immediate challenge is one of assessment validity. Traditional coding interviews test whether candidates can write algorithms from memory under time pressure—a skill of diminishing relevance when AI assistants can generate working code in seconds. Companies that continue using these assessments are increasingly finding that strong interview performers don't always make strong employees, while candidates who might excel in actual work environments struggle with artificial constraints. This disconnect is forcing a reconsideration of what interviews are meant to measure.

Some organizations have responded by explicitly allowing AI tool use during interviews, then assessing how candidates interact with the tools. This approach evaluates skills that arguably matter more in modern development: the ability to prompt effectively, to evaluate AI-generated code critically, to debug and improve upon suggestions, and to recognize when AI is producing incorrect or suboptimal solutions. Interviewers focus less on whether candidates can produce code and more on how they think about problems and refine solutions.

Other companies are moving in the opposite direction, implementing more restrictive interview environments to ensure they're assessing human capabilities. Proctored interviews conducted on locked-down machines eliminate AI assistance entirely. Some firms have returned to whiteboard interviews or verbal problem-solving discussions where AI tools cannot be easily used. These approaches acknowledge that candidates may face situations where AI assistance isn't available and baseline technical competence still matters.

The most innovative hiring practices are attempting to assess what makes humans valuable in an AI-augmented workplace. These focus on system design thinking, the ability to decompose complex problems, collaboration and communication skills, and judgment about when and how to apply different approaches. Take-home projects are being redesigned to require synthesis across multiple domains or to pose open-ended problems where there is no single correct answer that AI could provide. Some companies are introducing entirely new assessment formats designed specifically for the AI era.

Candidates are adapting to this shifting landscape. Top candidates are developing sophisticated skills for working with AI tools—skills that some employers value highly and others view skeptically. The rise of AI-assisted coding has also changed preparation strategies, with less emphasis on memorizing algorithms and more on understanding concepts deeply enough to evaluate AI-generated solutions. Career advisors are increasingly coaching candidates on how to discuss their AI tool usage thoughtfully during interviews.

The long-term implications extend beyond interview practices to fundamental questions about what technical roles will look like. If AI can handle increasingly complex coding tasks, the value of human engineers may shift toward activities that are harder to automate: understanding business context, making architectural decisions, mentoring, and navigating ambiguity. Companies that figure out how to hire for these capabilities—and interview processes that can assess them—will have significant advantages in building effective teams for an AI-augmented future.
